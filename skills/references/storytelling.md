# Storytelling Frameworks for Deck Creation

Use these rules when planning or editing decks. They are content-structure rules, not visual design rules.

An agent should apply them mechanically:

1. Clarify the audience, objective, recommendation, scope, and target length before drafting.
2. Decide the core answer first.
3. Organize supporting points into a small number of non-overlapping arguments.
4. Turn each slide into one clear message with evidence underneath it.
5. Remove framework labels from the final slide copy.

## Pyramid Principle

Start with the answer, not the background.

- Deck level:
  - executive summary = the answer or recommendation
  - sections = 2-4 supporting arguments
  - slides within each section = evidence, examples, or implications
- Slide level:
  - title = the answer for that slide
  - body = the evidence that proves the title

Mechanical rules:

- Write the recommendation before writing slide 1.
- Group support into 2-4 buckets that do not overlap materially.
- Put evidence under the argument it proves.
- If a point does not support the answer, cut it or move it.
- If a slide contains two messages, split it.

Deck example:

- Executive summary: "Expand into mid-market accounts because enterprise sales cycles are delaying growth."
- Section 1: "Enterprise pipeline conversion slowed after procurement requirements increased."
- Section 2: "Mid-market demand is large enough to offset slower enterprise growth."
- Section 3: "The current product and sales model can support a mid-market move with limited changes."

Slide example:

- Action title: "Enterprise conversion fell after new procurement gates extended cycle times."
- Body evidence:
  - win rate dropped from 24% to 16%
  - average cycle time rose from 72 to 109 days
  - three largest lost deals cited security review delays

## SCQA Framework

Use SCQA to shape the narrative flow:

- Situation: context the audience already recognizes
- Complication: what changed, broke, or became harder
- Question: the decision or strategic question created by the complication
- Answer: the recommendation or conclusion

Mechanical rules:

- Keep the order logical even if it spans multiple slides.
- Do not print labels such as "Situation:" or "Complication:" on slides.
- Make the question explicit in the outline even if it is implicit on the slide.
- Move quickly from context to the decision point; do not over-explain the situation.
- Ensure the answer resolves the question raised by the complication.

Outline example:

- Situation: "The product has grown steadily in SMB accounts for three years."
- Complication: "Growth slowed after competitors cut prices and copied core features."
- Question: "How should the company restore growth without compressing margins?"
- Answer: "Shift the next two quarters toward premium workflow automation instead of discounting."

Invisible slide flow example:

1. "SMB growth was stable until competitor price cuts reset buyer expectations."
2. "Price matching would protect volume but would erode gross margin below target."
3. "Workflow automation is the only differentiator customers still value enough to pay for."
4. "The roadmap should prioritize premium automation to restore growth without discounting."

## Action Titles

Every content slide title should state the takeaway as a complete sentence.

Use action titles instead of topic labels.

- Weak topic label: "Market Overview"
- Strong action title: "Market share fell 15% after the new entrant undercut pricing in Q2."

Mechanical rules:

- Make the title a full sentence with a verb.
- State the "so what," not the subject area.
- Keep one message per slide.
- Keep titles to a maximum of 2 lines, roughly 90 characters.
- Make the body prove the title with facts, logic, or examples.
- If the body does not prove the title, rewrite one of them.

Good title checks:

- Could a reader understand the takeaway from the title alone?
- Could every bullet, chart, or image on the slide be used as proof for that title?
- If not, the slide is not finished.

Rewrite example:

- Topic title: "Customer Churn"
- Better title: "Churn rose because onboarding delays left new customers inactive in week one."
- Supporting body:
  - activation rate fell from 68% to 49%
  - delayed integrations were present in 61% of churned accounts
  - cohorts with onboarding completed in 7 days retained 18 points better

## WWWH

Use WWWH before writing an outline.

- Who is the audience?
- Why are you telling this story?
- What is the key message?
- How should it be communicated?

Mechanical rules:

- Match vocabulary and detail level to the audience.
- Match the deck shape to the objective: decision, update, alignment, or approval.
- Reduce the deck to one key message before drafting sections.
- Choose the communication approach that best proves the message: comparison, timeline, driver tree, case example, or recommendation-first narrative.

WWWH example:

- Who: "VP of Sales and CFO"
- Why: "Decide whether to fund a new enablement program"
- What: "Fund the program because ramp time can be cut by 30% within two quarters"
- How: "Recommendation-first deck with baseline data, pilot evidence, economics, and decision ask"

## Pre-flight Questions

Ask or infer these before creating a deck:

1. Audience: who will view this deck?
2. Objective: what decision or takeaway is needed?
3. Recommendation: what is the core answer?
4. Scope: what is in and out of scope?
5. Deck length: what is the target slide count?

Mechanical rules:

- If the user provides all five answers, use them directly.
- If one or two are missing, make the smallest reasonable assumption and state it.
- If the recommendation is missing, do not default to a neutral summary deck; propose a recommendation candidate.
- If scope is broad, narrow it before generating slides.
- If deck length is unspecified, default to the shortest deck that can answer the question clearly.

Pre-flight example:

- Audience: "Executive staff"
- Objective: "Approve the 2027 hiring plan"
- Recommendation: "Hire 12 quota-carrying reps over two waves instead of 20 at once"
- Scope: "North America new business only; excludes renewals and Europe"
- Deck length: "7 slides"

Resulting outline shape:

1. Recommendation
2. Current capacity gap
3. Why the gap matters now
4. Why two waves beat a single large hiring round
5. Cost and payback evidence
6. Risks and mitigations
7. Decision required
